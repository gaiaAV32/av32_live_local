PARIS FASHION WEEK

 

The city’s most compelling collections

 
Last but by no means least, next season’s sartorial agenda isn’t set until Paris has its say. The only thing predictable about these designers is that their collections are going to be exciting – and seriously desirable.
 

Haider ACKERMANN

SHOP NOW >
 

1

The Royal Academy of Fine Arts Antwerp alumni has been seducing the style set with alluringly androgynous silhouettes and masterful colour treatment since 2003.

2

His cult collections are designed to be admired in motion. Sensuality balances fastidious tailoring while the protection implied by skillful drapery is tempered with romance.

3

Colombian born and adopted by French parents, the palpable clash of cultures in Ackermann’s work stems from his nomadic childhood and globetrotting lifestyle.
 
 
 
 
 
 

4

High-necked and low hemmed; the Austrian brightens up the fluid lines of his AW16 collection with yellow velvet and cornflower blue corduroy. That’s ’70s style evening and outerwear sorted then.

5

“The AW16 collection has a sophisticated feel. The inspiration comes from turn of the century Vienna and the contemporary Belgian painter Michaël Borremans,” Arbesser told us.

6

Regular visits to the Viennese opera sparked the selfconfessed nerd’s fascination with fashion and costume. He also references contemporary art and architecture, shown by his progressive presentations.
 
 

Y/PROJECT

SHOP NOW >
 

1

Founded in 2010 by Yohan Serfaty, architecture graduate turned fashion designer Glenn Martens became Y/Project’s Creative Director after his death in 2013.

2

Martens was Vetements founder Demna Gvasalia’s fit model while he studied at The Antwerp Royal Academy of Fine Arts and previously worked at Jean Paul Gaultier.

3

Named on the 2016 LVMH prize shortlist, Rihanna is currently rocking Y/Project on her Anti World Tour and Swiss stylist Ursina Gysi has already been streetstyle snapped in the brand.
 
 

4

AW16 shows off Martens’ melting pot of historical and contemporary references. Ruching adds subversive sex appeal to velvet and denim while bishop sleeves give tops major impact.

5

Proposing an experimental blend of menswear and womenswear, the off-beat femininity the Belgian achieves is impeccably tailored and revolves around elongated silhouettes and ingenious details.

6

“We take any reference we like regardless of era or subculture. At the end we just want to have fun and make fun clothes.” Martens told us.
 
 

KOCHÉ

SHOP NOW >
 

1

One of the stars shining brightest in Paris’s new guard of designers, Christelle Kocher’s brand Koché has already made the LVMH prize shortlist twice since launching in 2014.

2

Based upon Kocher’s concept of ‘couture to wear’, the collections clash urban streetwear with contemporary art references and showcase Kocher’s incredible technical ability.

3

The Central Saint Martins trained designer is also Artistic Director at illustrious plumassier Maison Lemarié. Her impressive CV includes stints at Emporio Armani, Dries Van Noten and Bottega Veneta.
 
 

4

Zooming into Koché’s fluid and flattering creations for AW16: contrasting textures and photographic prints are patch-worked together while assorted beads stud rich velvet.

5

“The idea behind Koché is to mix [the] everyday with French couture and artisanal work,” Kocher told wmagazine.com. “Like having a really amazing dress, but mixing it with jogging pants.”

6

Kocher’s non-elitist approach extends to her presentations. Open to the public and held in unorthodox venues to reveal the real Paris, the models are friends or street-cast.
 
 

COURRÈGES

SHOP NOW >
 

1

The late André Courrèges launched his eponymous brand in 1961; former Balenciaga designers Arnaud Vaillant and Sébastien Meyer took over the reins in May 2015.

2

Masters of modern minimalism, the Parisian duo helmed their own ANDAM prize winning label Coperni Femme from 2013 until 2015, shutting it to focus on Courrèges.

3

Forward-thinking as ever, Vaillant and Meyer are simply revamping the house’s space-age spirit, structured silhouettes and futuristic fabrications for a 21st century audience
 
 

4

For their bolder AW16 offering, the duo used diamond cut-outs, monochrome square prints and more to decorate boxy jackets, day-to-night dresses and standout miniskirts.

5

“It’s a product-based approach, which lies at the heart of our concept. Our idea is to make cool, wearable, young clothes that can be renewed season after season,” Meyer told numero.com.

6

Worn by Audrey Hepburn and Catherine Deneuve in its initial heyday, Amal Clooney, Alexa Chung and Leandra Medine are modern-day Courrèges’ girls.
