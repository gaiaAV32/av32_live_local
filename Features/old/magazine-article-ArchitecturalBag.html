Architecture is perhaps the most moving contribution we have made to the landscape around us, also employing craftsmanship of the highest order. And the starchitects, the eminent roster of today’s foremost architects, are as bankable as the greats of the contemporary art world. They’re as famous too. So then, when accessories designer Nathalie Trad conceived her AW15 collection of structurally brilliant clutches, how could they not be compared to the buildings we long to look at?

THE ELIFERIE & FOSTER + PARTNERS

— British architect Norman Foster and his practice were chosen to design Canary Wharf’s Crossrail station, set to open in 2018. The aerodynamic encasing, made of speciality plastic ETFE and timber, forms a futuristic sail that is not too dissimilar to the spindly structure of Trad’s Eliferia clutch.

THE HUGO & FRANK GEHRY

— Nathalie Trad’s Hugo bag, with its checkboard of pearl and resin, is reminiscent of the billowing sheet of glass and metal that is Frank Gehry’s IAC building in Chelsea, New York. A retrospective exhibition on Gehry’s work so far has just opened at Fondation Louis Vuitton in Paris, which he also designed.

THE KLEIN BAG & ZAHA HADID

— The swooping concrete curves and bisecting lines of France’s Pierres Vives Building, a governmental institution designed by modernist maven Zaha Hadid and completed in 2012, can be seen in the copper struts of Trad’s pitch blackminaudière. Notice the texture too, the bag’s resin-finished shell case could almost be cast concrete...

THE COLLINS & REM KOOHAAS AND OLE SCHEEREN

— China’s CCTV Headquarters (Chinese Central Television HQ) is a gravity-defying feat of engineering thought up by Rem Koolhaas and Ole Scheeren. The building’s latticework, which is etched all over the 44-story skyscraper, could be the black cubic framework etching out Nathalie Trad’s Collins.

THE RUBIN BAG & JOHN LAUTNER

— The work of American architect John Lautner, a favourite of Louis Vuitton’s Nicholas Ghesquière, is reflected in the copper tessellations of the Rubin bag. Lautner’s space-age roof is one of the most compelling features of the building it comes from: The Sheats-Goldstein Residence.
